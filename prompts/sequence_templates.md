# Email Sequence Templates

These archetypes guide Scout when building sequences. Each one provides structural guidance —
Scout personalizes using Steven's voice profile and the specific campaign context.

All sequences use Outreach.io merge fields: {{first_name}}, {{district}}, {{title}}

# SECTION 1: COLD PROSPECTING BY ROLE

---

## ARCHETYPE: Cold Prospecting — CS / STEM / CTE Directors & Coordinators

**Target:** CS Director, STEM Director, CTE Director, CS Coordinator, STEM Coordinator,
EdTech Director, Director of Educational Technology, Director of Innovation,
Director of Digital Learning, Computer Science Coordinator, Instructional Technology Director

**Core angle:** CodeCombat is the most complete K-12 CS + AI suite available — and it's built to
make CS/STEM directors look good to their districts. Turnkey curriculum, built-in pacing, real
student engagement data. This person owns the decision or has strong influence. Appeal to their
professional identity as a CS champion.

**Sequence structure:**
- Step 1 (Day 0): Short cold intro. Acknowledge their specific role ("you're one of the few people in any district who actually drives CS strategy"). Reference one real pain point — teacher prep, student engagement, AP enrollment, AI literacy. Single CTA: quick call or reply.
- Step 2 (Day 3): Value-add only. Share a specific metric, case study, or product insight relevant to their grade bands or focus area. Don't ask for anything.
- Step 3 (Day 7): Peer proof. Reference what a similar CS director at a comparable district is doing. Name a state if relevant.
- Step 4 (Day 12): Pivot to a product they likely haven't considered (AI HackStack, AP CSP, AI League). Short — just open a new door.
- Step 5 (Day 18): "Still the right time?" light check-in. Low pressure.
- Step 6+ (if needed): LinkedIn touch note, or re-engage with a new resource/grant/policy angle.

**Tone:** Peer-to-peer. Steven knows CS ed deeply. Write like a colleague, not a vendor rep.
Avoid "I wanted to reach out" and "just checking in."

## ARCHETYPE: Cold Prospecting — Curriculum Directors / CAOs / Directors of Instruction

**Target:** Curriculum Director, Director of Curriculum, Chief Academic Officer, CAO,
Director of Curriculum & Instruction, Director of Teaching & Learning, Director of Academic Programs,
Assistant Superintendent of Curriculum, Director of Instruction, Curriculum Specialist

**Core angle:** CS is increasingly a curriculum mandate, not optional. AI readiness is a board-level
question. CodeCombat handles implementation complexity so curriculum teams don't have to build
from scratch. Appeal to standards alignment, teacher support, and scalability.

**Sequence structure:**
- Step 1 (Day 0): Lead with the curriculum mandate angle — CSTA standards, state CS requirements,
  AI literacy pressure from boards and parents. One sentence on what CodeCombat solves. CTA: call.
- Step 2 (Day 4): Standards alignment deep-dive. CSTA, ISTE, state-specific if known.
  Mention the turnkey teacher resources (no heavy PD lift).
- Step 3 (Day 9): ROI / cost-efficiency angle. One platform across K-12 instead of multiple point solutions.
- Step 4 (Day 15): Teacher adoption angle — scaffold, pacing guides, built-in differentiation.
- Step 5 (Day 22): Soft close / delegate. "Is CS under your team's umbrella, or should I be talking to someone else?"

**Tone:** Strategic and outcome-oriented. Curriculum directors think in terms of scope and sequence,
teacher support, and vertical alignment. Mirror that language.

## ARCHETYPE: Cold Prospecting — Teachers

**Target:** CS Teacher, Computer Science Teacher, Coding Teacher, AP CSP Teacher, AP CSA Teacher,
AP Computer Science Teacher, STEM Teacher, Robotics Teacher, Esports Teacher, Game Design Teacher,
Web Design Teacher, Engineering Teacher, Makerspace Coordinator, Instructional Technology Coach,
TOSA, Digital Learning Coach

**Core angle:** Teachers are influencers, not final buyers — but they're often the most powerful
advocates. The angle is: "CodeCombat makes your class more engaging and your job easier." Free
teacher accounts, student engagement data, zero prep time. If they love it, they'll push it up.

**Sequence structure:**
- Step 1 (Day 0): Super short. "Your students will actually want to code." Reference one thing that
  makes CodeCombat different from what they're probably using (game-based, no-login student access,
  Python/JS/Lua from day one). Offer a free teacher account or demo.
- Step 2 (Day 4): Specific to their subject if known (AP CSP: full CB-approved course;
  Robotics: AI League tournaments; Makerspace: project-based AI curriculum).
- Step 3 (Day 10): Student outcome angle — engagement, completion rates, equity.
  "Works for students who never thought coding was for them."
- Step 4 (Day 17): "Who do I talk to about getting licenses for your whole class / school?"
  Invite them to make the internal introduction.

**Tone:** Warm, energetic, teacher-first. They get a lot of vendor emails. Be the one who actually
understands what their classroom looks like. No corporate speak.

## ARCHETYPE: Cold Prospecting — Principals / Assistant Principals

**Target:** Principal, Assistant Principal, Vice Principal, Head of School

**Core angle:** Principals care about student outcomes, teacher retention, and community standing.
The CS/AI angle is increasingly about workforce readiness and school reputation — parents and boards
are asking "what are you doing about AI?" CodeCombat gives them a visible, credible answer.

**Sequence structure:**
- Step 1 (Day 0): 3 sentences max. Lead with a question or data point that hits their world:
  "What does your CS program look like right now?" or "Districts that add structured CS programs
  see measurable increases in student engagement." One ask.
- Step 2 (Day 5): Teacher angle — "Turnkey curriculum means teachers don't need CS background."
  Lowers their biggest internal objection.
- Step 3 (Day 11): Community / parent optics. "AI readiness is the new STEM — parents and boards
  are asking about it at every district." Reference local/state CS mandates if known.
- Step 4 (Day 18): Soft delegation. "Is there a CS coordinator or curriculum lead I should loop in?"
  Help them connect you to the right person.
- Step 5 (Day 25): Final touch. "Leaving you alone after this — but wanted to make sure this wasn't
  lost in the inbox."

**Tone:** Respect their time. They're generalists managing a hundred things. Be direct and make
the value obvious in the first sentence.

## ARCHETYPE: Cold Prospecting — Superintendents

**Target:** Superintendent, Assistant Superintendent, Deputy Superintendent, Chief Education Officer

**Core angle:** ROI, strategic differentiation, and community/board optics. Supers are thinking
about district-wide impact, budget justification, and the "AI readiness" question from parents
and board members. Keep it short — they have assistants screening email.

**Sequence structure:**
- Step 1 (Day 0): 3 sentences. Headline outcome ("Districts using CodeCombat see CS enrollment
  double in year 2"). One sentence on who CodeCombat is. One ask.
- Step 2 (Day 4): Student achievement / equity angle — CS access gaps, underrepresented students,
  Title I alignment if relevant.
- Step 3 (Day 9): Teacher retention / satisfaction angle — low PD burden, teachers love using it.
- Step 4 (Day 15): Board optics / workforce readiness. "What's your district's AI readiness story?"
- Step 5 (Day 22): Soft close / delegate. "Happy to connect with whoever leads curriculum
  or CS programs if that's a better fit."

**Tone:** Executive brevity. Lead with outcomes. No jargon. Never more than 4 sentences per email.

## ARCHETYPE: License Request — US Schools (Inbound)

**Trigger:** A teacher, coordinator, or admin submitted a license request form or emailed asking
about pricing/access. They raised their hand — this is the warmest possible lead.

**Core angle:** Speed and ease. They want this. Remove every friction point. Get to a number
and a next step fast.

**Sequence structure:**
- Step 1 (Day 0): Same day if possible. Acknowledge the request by name, confirm you saw it,
  ask one qualifying question: grade level, number of students, specific product interest.
  Don't send a wall of information yet.
- Step 2 (Day 1): Based on their answer (or if no response), send a tailored one-pager or
  product recommendation. Include a link to a demo or free trial.
- Step 3 (Day 3): Follow up with pricing or a quote. "Here's what this typically looks like
  for a district your size." Keep it simple — range is fine, exact quote comes on the call.
- Step 4 (Day 7): "Still interested? Happy to jump on a 15-minute call to walk through options."
- Step 5 (Day 14): Final check-in. "Don't want to lose you — is this still on your radar?"

**Tone:** Warm, responsive, fast. They came to you. Be helpful, not sales-y.
Never make them wait more than 24 hours for first contact.

## ARCHETYPE: License Request — International (Canada, Mexico, South America)

**Trigger:** Inbound license request from a school or district outside the US
(Canada, Mexico, Colombia, Brazil, Argentina, Chile, Peru, other LATAM).

**Core angle:** Same urgency as US license requests, but with additional sensitivity to:
- Currency (USD pricing may feel high — emphasize value and ROI)
- Language needs (Spanish/French versions, bilingual support)
- Time zones and local school calendars
- Potential for country-level or regional partnerships

**Sequence structure:**
- Step 1 (Day 0): Respond same day. Acknowledge where they're from, confirm products available
  for their region. Ask: language preference for students, grade level, size.
- Step 2 (Day 2): Send localized product recommendation. Mention any Spanish-language content
  (CodeCombat Junior, Ozaria have translated content). Address currency/pricing clearly.
- Step 3 (Day 5): Social proof from nearby country or region if available.
  If not, use US case study and contextualize ("similar-sized district").
- Step 4 (Day 10): Propose a call. Offer flexible times across time zones.
- Step 5 (Day 18): Final follow-up. Keep door open for next semester.

**Tone:** Warm, internationally aware. Don't assume US context. Be explicit about what's
available in their region. Avoid idioms that don't translate.

## ARCHETYPE: License Request — Libraries

**Trigger:** A library or library system requested information about licensing CodeCombat
for their programs (after-school, coding clubs, open computer time, summer programs).

**Core angle:** Libraries often have different budget structures and timelines than schools.
They may use grants, E-rate, or LSTA funding. The use case is usually self-directed / after-school,
so ease of use and self-pacing matter most.

**Sequence structure:**
- Step 1 (Day 0): Acknowledge request, ask: is this for a school library, public library, or
  library system? How many locations? What's the primary use case (coding club, open access, summer)?
- Step 2 (Day 2): Product recommendation based on their answer. For coding clubs → CodeCombat
  Classroom or Junior. For open/self-directed → Ozaria or AI Junior. Include pricing overview.
- Step 3 (Day 6): Funding angle. "Many library programs fund CodeCombat through Title IV-A,
  E-rate, LSTA, or digital equity grants. Happy to share documentation if that helps."
- Step 4 (Day 12): "Want to hop on a quick call? I can walk you through exactly what other
  libraries are doing and get you a quote."
- Step 5 (Day 20): Final touch.

**Tone:** Practical and grant-savvy. Libraries run lean. Acknowledge budget realities directly.

## ARCHETYPE: Quote Follow-Up

**Trigger:** Steven sent a formal quote or pricing proposal and hasn't heard back.

**Core angle:** Move the deal forward. Understand blockers. Most quote stalls are budget,
timing, or committee — not disinterest. Surface the real objection so you can address it.

**Sequence structure:**
- Step 1 (Day 2 post-quote): Simple check-in. "Wanted to make sure the quote came through
  clearly — any questions on the numbers?" Offer to adjust scope, timing, or structure.
- Step 2 (Day 6): Address the most common objection for their role:
  - Curriculum directors: "Does the quote align with your budget cycle?"
  - Supers: "Is there a board approval process I should know about?"
  - CS directors: "Any questions on per-seat pricing vs. site license?"
- Step 3 (Day 12): Introduce urgency where real — semester start, pilot window, pricing validity.
  "This quote is valid through [date] — just want to flag before we get into crunch time."
- Step 4 (Day 20): "Happy to revise the scope if the full quote is a stretch right now.
  A pilot or phase-one approach might be an easier internal sell."
- Step 5 (Day 30): Final check. "Still interested? I'll archive this if I don't hear back —
  but happy to revisit whenever the timing works."

**Tone:** Confident, not needy. These are real deals — move with purpose but don't chase.

## ARCHETYPE: Webinar Follow-Up

**Trigger:** The prospect attended (or registered for) a CodeCombat webinar.

**Core angle:** They showed up — that's a signal. The goal is to move from passive interest
to an active conversation while the content is still fresh.

**Sequence structure:**
- Step 1 (Day 1 post-webinar): Reference the specific webinar by name. Ask one question tied
  to the content: "What was most relevant to what you're working on?" or "Did the [specific
  feature covered] resonate with your situation?" Avoid generic "thanks for attending."
- Step 2 (Day 4): Share a resource that extends what the webinar covered — case study,
  product deep-dive, or relevant blog post.
- Step 3 (Day 9): Propose a live demo or pilot conversation. "Now that you've seen the overview,
  want to see how it maps to your specific district?"
- Step 4 (Day 16): Light check-in. "Still thinking about it? Happy to answer questions async
  if a call isn't the right format right now."
- Step 5 (Day 25): Final follow-up.

**Tone:** Warm and specific to the webinar topic. Don't make it feel like a mass follow-up blast.
Reference the content — they'll notice.

## ARCHETYPE: Conference Follow-Up

**Trigger:** Steven met this person at a conference (CSTA, ISTE, SXSWedu, state CS conference,
CUE, FETC, etc.) and had an in-person or virtual conversation.

**Core angle:** Lean hard on the in-person connection. They already know Steven's face.
Move fast while the energy is fresh — conference leads go cold within 2 weeks.

**Sequence structure:**
- Step 1 (Day 1-2 post-conference): Reference the specific conference, the conversation you had,
  and one specific thing they mentioned (their district, their CS program, a product they were
  curious about). Make it unmistakably personal. Propose a follow-up call.
- Step 2 (Day 5): Share what you promised in person (or if nothing was promised, share the
  most relevant resource based on what they care about).
- Step 3 (Day 10): Pilot or demo proposal. "Based on what you told me about [their situation],
  here's what I'd recommend as a starting point."
- Step 4 (Day 18): "Still thinking about it? Happy to reconnect whenever works."
- Step 5 (Day 28): Final touch before archiving.

**Tone:** Personal and warm. This is a continuation of a real conversation.
Don't let it feel like a template — every conference follow-up should feel custom.

## ARCHETYPE: Closed-Lost Re-Engagement

**Trigger:** An opportunity was lost or closed in the last 18 months. Budget, timing, competitor,
internal decision — whatever the reason, the door may be open again now.

**Core angle:** Things change. Budget cycles reset. The competitor they chose might not have
delivered. New leadership might have new priorities. Show up with something new and relevant —
not a reminder of the past.

**Sequence structure:**
- Step 1 (Day 0): Acknowledge the gap without dwelling on it. "It's been a while since we talked.
  A lot has changed on our end — we launched [new product/feature] and I thought of you."
  Keep it short. No guilt, no pressure.
- Step 2 (Day 5): Lead with what's new — product launch, case study from their region,
  policy/grant development relevant to them. Be genuinely useful.
- Step 3 (Day 11): Direct question about their current situation. "Is CS still a priority
  this year, or did the landscape change?" Give them an easy way to tell you where they are.
- Step 4 (Day 19): One more useful resource or invite. Keep door open.
- Step 5 (Day 30): Final touch. "I'll leave it here for now — reach out any time."

**Tone:** No guilt. No "just following up from 18 months ago." Fresh energy, new value.
Don't make them feel awkward about having said no before.

## ARCHETYPE: Re-Engagement (Cold Lead — Went Silent)

**Trigger:** A prospect showed early interest (replied, downloaded something, had a call,
attended a demo) but went cold — no response in 60-120+ days.

**Core angle:** Timing might have been off. Life gets busy. Reach back with something fresh
and relevant — not a "you haven't responded" reminder.

**Sequence structure:**
- Step 1 (Day 0): Brief, no-pressure note. Reference the prior touchpoint once, lightly.
  "We talked last [semester/fall] about CodeCombat for your district — I remember you were
  interested in [product/use case]. Checking in to see if the timing is better now."
- Step 2 (Day 5): Share something new — product update, case study, grant opportunity,
  or policy development. Give them a reason to engage beyond just "are you ready yet?"
- Step 3 (Day 11): Ask a direct question tied to their situation: "What does your CS program
  look like heading into next year?" Lower the bar for them to reply.
- Step 4 (Day 20): Soft close. "Happy to reconnect or send some resources async — just let me
  know what's most useful."

**Tone:** Light, respectful of their time. Don't make them feel chased. One hand extended,
not two hands pulling.

## ARCHETYPE: Generic Cold Prospecting (Any Role)

**Use when:** The target role is unclear, it's a broad prospecting push, or Steven hasn't
specified a specific role archetype.

**Core angle:** Lead with the most universal value prop — CodeCombat is the only K-12
platform that covers CS, AI, and game-based engagement in one suite, K-12. Every school
needs this. Find their specific pain point with a question.

**Sequence structure:**
- Step 1 (Day 0): Short intro with a specific question: "What does CS or coding look like
  at {{district}} right now?" Lead with curiosity, not product.
- Step 2 (Day 4): Brief product overview tied to their likely use case (inferred from title/district).
  One clear differentiator vs. generic alternatives.
- Step 3 (Day 9): Case study or outcome data from a similar district or state.
- Step 4 (Day 15): Product pivot — introduce something unexpected (AI League, AP CSP, AI HackStack).
- Step 5 (Day 22): "Still the right time?" low-pressure check-in.
- Step 6 (Day 32): Final touch before archiving.

**Tone:** Curious and helpful first, salesy never. Let the prospect define the conversation.

## ARCHETYPE: District Penetration (Existing Interest / Usage / Sales)

**Trigger:** The district already has some CodeCombat presence — a pilot, an existing license,
a teacher who's using it, or a recent sale. Goal is to expand usage and revenue within the district.

**Core angle:** Start from "you're already seeing results" — build on momentum rather than
starting from scratch. The warmth here is real. Reference what they have, what's working,
and what the natural next step is (more seats, new grade levels, additional products).

**Sequence structure:**
- Step 1 (Day 0): Reference the existing relationship specifically — what product, how long,
  what you know about their usage. Ask about results: "How has it been going with your students?"
  This is a check-in, not a pitch.
- Step 2 (Day 4): Based on their current product, introduce the natural expansion:
  - Using Classroom? → Add Ozaria or AP CSP.
  - Using Junior? → Bridge to Classroom for middle school.
  - Using one school? → District-wide license saves money per seat.
  - Teachers using it? → Get admin buy-in for official adoption.
- Step 3 (Day 9): ROI / efficiency angle. "A district license at your size would cost
  X per student — here's how that compares to what you're paying now."
- Step 4 (Day 16): Introduce a new product they haven't tried — AI HackStack, AI League,
  AP CSP if they don't have it.
- Step 5 (Day 24): Propose a review call. "Would love to look at usage data with you and
  figure out what makes sense for next year."

**Tone:** Collegial, consultative. They're already a customer or warm contact. Don't sell
from scratch — sell from where you already are.

## ARCHETYPE: Pilot Sequences — Spring Semester

**Trigger:** Steven is pitching a spring pilot program — short-term, low-commitment, typically
January–May. Common at end of year when districts want to "try before they buy" for fall renewal.

**Core angle:** Spring is the perfect time to run a low-risk pilot before making a full
commitment for next year. Fast implementation, real data, easy internal sell. Creates urgency
because spring semesters have a hard end date.

**Sequence structure:**
- Step 1 (Day 0): Introduce the spring pilot framing directly. "With spring semester underway,
  now is the ideal time to run a CodeCombat pilot — you'll have real student data by May to
  justify a district-wide license for fall." CTA: pilot proposal call.
- Step 2 (Day 3): Specifics of what a pilot looks like — timeline, seat count, onboarding,
  teacher support, reporting. Make it feel low-lift and low-risk.
- Step 3 (Day 8): Reference a similar district that piloted in spring and expanded in fall.
  Concrete numbers if possible.
- Step 4 (Day 14): Urgency. "Spring semester is moving fast — if we're starting in [month],
  we'd need to get setup done by [date]." Real deadlines only.
- Step 5 (Day 21): Flexible close. "If spring doesn't work, we can set something up for
  the start of next year — but spring gives you more time to evaluate."
- Step 6 (Day 30): Final follow-up.

**Tone:** Helpful urgency — there's a real semester clock here. Don't manufacture pressure,
but do be clear about the timeline reality.

# A/B TESTING GUIDANCE

When building a sequence, Scout can generate A/B variants. Best candidates:
- **Subject lines:** Curiosity vs. specific benefit vs. name-drop vs. question
- **Step 1 opener:** Pain-first vs. outcome-first vs. question-first vs. social proof first
- **CTA framing:** "15-minute call" vs. "quick reply" vs. "would this be useful?"
- **Email length:** Long (detail-oriented prospects) vs. short (executive-level prospects)

Label variant A as primary, variant B as alternative. Steven picks which to test.
When building for a specific role, default to generating variants for steps 1, 2, and 3.
